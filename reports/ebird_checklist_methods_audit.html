The correct data flow

1. Define independent events

Collapse shared submissions by group identifier; keep the component checklist and observer IDs for audit.

2. Build the denominator

Filter the SED to eligible, complete sampling events using the same release and rules as the EBD.

3. Reconstruct responses

Join reported taxa, infer non-detection only on complete checklists, and keep “X” out of numeric count models.

4. Attach exposure and model

Assign herring exposure at a scale compatible with checklist footprint; account for effort, repeated observers/sites, and grouped validation.

1. Shared checklists are the clearest pseudoreplication risk

eBird permits each participant in a group to own a copy of the same field event. The group_identifier connects those copies. Cornell’s auk_unique() selects a single component record (the lowest checklist ID) and retains all contributing IDs; it does not adjudicate a scientific disagreement among edited copies.

The Stage 2 repair now constructs one composite event per group, excludes effort-disagreement groups from the primary frame, and retains them for a registered sensitivity. The remaining gate is human acceptance plus executable proof that one group produces exactly one checklist-species event.

2. Checklist coordinates do not describe a 10 km route

Public EBD/SED data provide one coordinate. A hotspot coordinate may differ from the observer’s exact position, and a traveling list covers a route whose public geometry is unavailable. Cornell’s own fine-scale guidance says that stricter travel-distance filtering is needed as spatial precision increases.

For this project the concern is directional: a long checklist whose point lies within 2 km of spawn may include many observations farther away, while a point outside 2 km may have traversed exposed shoreline. This tends to blur a true local effect and can also distort distance gradients.

3. Non-detection is not absence

For an eligible complete checklist, failure to report a focal species supports an inferred checklist-level non-detection. It does not show that the species was absent from the shoreline, because detectability varies with species, observer, time, effort, weather, distance, and group behavior.

detection estimand = P(species reported | eligible complete checklist, exposure, modeled observation process)

4. Counts are relative indices

The positive-count component estimates the number reported when detected and numerically counted. It is not a census of all birds present. A hurdle lognormal can be reasonable for extreme congregatory counts, but should earn its role through blocked predictive checks against truncated NB2, especially in the upper tail.

positive-count estimand = E(reported numeric count | detected, count available, eligible checklist)

5. Preferential sampling needs a denominator diagnostic

Birders may preferentially visit conspicuous herring events. Complete checklists and effort adjustment do not remove this site-selection process. The most parsimonious check is response-blind: compare numbers of checklists and unique observers, protocol, duration, distance, party size, and start-time availability across frozen exposure time/distance cells.

This is a sampling-support diagnostic, not a new adjustment set. If spawn-proximate support comes mainly from a few observers or unusual protocols, narrow the estimand or the eligible-checklist set.

6. Avoid accidental occupancy claims

Standard occupancy models require repeat visits to sites and closure assumptions. Most eBird data are single visits, and treating different points in an area as repeat visits can bias occupancy when sampled locations are not representative. The locked detection model should therefore be described as checklist encounter/reporting unless a separate, justified repeat-visit occupancy design is used.

Minimal recommendations—no variable bloat

  1. Verify one-event-per-group deduplication. Preserve protected component IDs and disagreement state, but never treat shared copies as independent checklists.
  2. Resolve shoreline coverage first. Repair the coastwide shoreline source, narrow the primary geography, or approve another source geometry and rerun the outcome-blind gate.
  3. Resolve checklist spatial scale at the gate. Retain a stationary/traveling ≤2 km high-precision analysis around the repaired ≤5 km, ≤10-observer candidate primary.
  4. Run one metadata-only sampling-support audit. Use checklist counts, unique observers and existing effort fields across the already-frozen exposure cells. Do not read bird responses.
  5. Lock plain-language estimands. “Probability reported on an eligible complete checklist” and “reported count conditional on detection” are defensible; population abundance and true occupancy are not.
  6. Use blocked validation. Hold out complete herring event complexes or shoreline-time blocks, not random checklist rows.

Scope and limitation

This is a design and literature audit reconciled to the approved Stage 2 gate. Protected row-level data were not copied into this report. Human choices are recorded, but executable QA and separate Stage 3 authorization remain necessary before response analysis.

This companion belongs with the broader Pacific herring × eBird literature survey. It does not alter the frozen candidate grid or fit a registered response model.
