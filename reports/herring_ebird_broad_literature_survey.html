Broad literature survey for the Pacific herring x eBird project

A disciplined expansion of the literature base, organized around the claims the locked analyses can actually address. The review broadens interpretation and method support without turning every plausible driver into a new model variable.

Bottom line

The literature base is now strong enough to support the existing question and model families. The expansion adds important modern work on herring phenology and spatial redistribution, eBird bias correction, structured-survey integration, and BC coastal-waterbird trends. It does not reveal a missing covariate that must be added to the primary analysis.

The most useful change is bibliographic and interpretive: cite each evidence stream for the claim it can support, and keep causal language proportional to the design. One operational guardrail should be added: do not combine raw British Columbia Coastal Waterbird Survey (BCCWS) records with eBird until checklist-level overlap can be identified.

How the literature maps to the scientific argument

1. Herring measurement

Defines where, when, and how much spawn was observed - including survey-era uncertainty.

2. Bird response

Counts and telemetry establish aggregation, movement, and timing near active spawn.

3. Mechanism

Diet and foraging studies show that roe use can plausibly generate the observed response.

4. eBird inference

Checklist, effort, observer, and sampling-bias methods protect the response estimate.

Strongly supported

  • Pacific herring spawn is a brief, concentrated resource pulse used by many coastal birds.
  • Scoters and Harlequin Ducks can aggregate and move substantial distances to exploit spawn.
  • Event timing and shoreline location are more biologically aligned than region-wide annual biomass alone.
  • eBird inference improves materially when restricted to complete checklists and adjusted for effort and observer heterogeneity.
  • Herring spawn observations have important method-era and spatial-measurement caveats.

Still unresolved

  • Whether eBird can recover a coastwide event-scale response with the same clarity as dedicated surveys.
  • Whether response magnitude tracks spawn biomass monotonically across species and regions.
  • How much bird redistribution is movement among nearby sites versus long-distance recruitment.
  • Whether short aggregative responses translate into demographic benefits.
  • What proportion of BCCWS checklists has also been exported to eBird.

BCCWS and eBird: the duplication issue

Birds Canada documents that NatureCounts users can enable automatic export of app-entered checklists to their eBird account. BCCWS is one of the checklist protocols supported by NatureCounts. Therefore, some BCCWS observations may exist in both systems. Public documentation does not state what fraction was exported and does not expose a universal public crosswalk.

Recommendation: published BCCWS trend studies are safe and valuable as background literature. Raw BCCWS data should not be pooled with eBird as an independent response stream unless Birds Canada can provide eBird checklist identifiers or another deterministic deduplication key. Without that, use BCCWS only in a separate validation analysis and explicitly acknowledge possible record overlap.

Review method and limitations

This is a broad scoping review, not a PRISMA systematic review or meta-analysis. Searches emphasized peer-reviewed primary studies and official Canadian/eBird documentation through 20 July 2026, then used backwards and forwards citation chasing around the major direct herring-bird papers, herring survey methods, eBird inference, BC coastal-waterbird trends, and resource-pulse theory. The attached review was inventoried first so the supplement could emphasize genuinely additive sources.

No response-bearing project data were accessed and no registered model was fitted. Literature was classified by the claim it can support, not by whether it would make the model more elaborate. Some older reports lack DOI metadata; their stable repository or journal landing page is supplied instead. Absence of a paper from this map should not be interpreted as evidence that it does not exist.

Prepared as a literature supplement to the Stage 2 outcome-blind design lock. Scientific interpretation remains subject to human review.
