# Scientific framework and hypotheses

## Central ecological proposition

Pacific herring spawning is a short-lived, spatially concentrated marine resource pulse. The main expectation is not simply that every species' probability of appearing within a 5-km circle rises after a single recorded date. A richer set of mechanisms is plausible:

- birds may aggregate before the recorded start date;
- some guilds eat roe, others target adult fish or associated prey;
- response timing and spatial scale should differ among guilds;
- flock size may rise strongly while encounter probability changes little;
- local increases may be paired with decreases elsewhere if birds redistribute;
- large or extensive spawn events should produce stronger or broader responses;
- observers may also redistribute toward visible spawn events.

Version 2 tests these mechanisms separately.

## Prespecified hypotheses

### H1 — Local aggregation

Herring-associated species and guilds show increased encounter probability, reported count, richness, aggregation-exceedance probability, or community share near active spawn relative to appropriate baseline periods.

**Supportive pattern:** positive event-time effects inside near rings, with uncertainty excluding or concentrating above a biologically meaningful null for at least one prespecified response scale.

### H2 — Distance decay

The response is strongest within the first few kilometres and weakens with distance.

**Supportive pattern:** a decreasing continuous exposure-response curve, a monotonic or near-monotonic ring gradient, or stronger additive-kernel predictions at shorter spatial scales.

### H3 — Temporal pulse

Responses have biologically interpretable timing rather than a generic spring trend.

**Supportive pattern:** a localized peak around spawn start/egg availability, robustness to calendar adjustment, and failure of shifted-date placebos.

### H4 — Redistribution

Increases near spawn are accompanied by smaller increases or decreases at simultaneous comparison shorelines and outer distance rings.

**Supportive pattern:** a positive near-versus-outer difference-in-changes; increased regional allocation share near spawn; or negative outer-ring changes concurrent with positive near-ring changes.

### H5 — Intensity and extent

Larger relative spawn index, greater spawn length/width, or greater additive exposure from multiple events predicts larger or broader bird responses.

**Supportive pattern:** positive dose-response or interaction with event time, bounded within comparable method/component support.

### H6 — Functional mechanisms

Roe-diving sea ducks, roe-feeding gulls, and intertidal roe feeders respond most during egg availability, while piscivores and scavengers may peak earlier or more broadly.

**Supportive pattern:** guild-level timing differences aligned with feeding mechanism, with species deviations retained.

### H7 — Community reorganization

The composition and diversity of the nearshore bird assemblage changes during spawn.

**Supportive pattern:** blocked multivariate separation, changes in guild richness or Hill diversity, and coherent species loadings.

### H8 — Observation-process separation

A biological signal remains after modeling checklist effort, observer experience, repeated observers, locality, calendar, and observer visitation/allocation.

**Supportive pattern:** similar ecological contrasts in same-observer, same-location, and calendar-matched designs; visitation effects do not fully reproduce bird-response patterns.

## Estimand families

Version 2 keeps estimands separate rather than averaging incompatible answers:

1. **Checklist encounter:** probability a taxon is reported on an eligible complete checklist.
2. **Positive reported flock size:** expected numeric count conditional on a positive numeric report.
3. **Unconditional reported count:** combined zero and positive response; still not absolute abundance.
4. **Aggregation probability:** probability of a species-specific large-flock threshold.
5. **Guild response:** any detection, richness, summed reported count, or upper-tail event among guild members.
6. **Community response:** multivariate composition and diversity.
7. **Redistribution:** near-versus-outer or spawn-versus-comparison difference-in-changes on identical calendar support.
8. **Observer allocation:** conditional probability that a submitted checklist occurs at a spawn-associated shoreline within an observed choice set.

## Evidence policy

The purpose of multiple models is triangulation, not selecting the one that produces the desired sign. All prespecified models are retained in the results registry. Strong support requires agreement across at least two independent design families—for example, event-time/distance and calendar-matched redistribution—not merely several variants of the same model.

Null or negative results are reported. A negative near-spawn result can itself indicate early arrival, within-radius redistribution, habitat mismatch, observer selection, or a genuinely absent response; those explanations must be tested rather than assumed.
