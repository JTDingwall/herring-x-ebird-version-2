# Stage 4A v2 editorial materials

## Plain-language summary

Each spring, Pacific herring lay eggs in shallow coastal waters, creating a short-lived food pulse for birds and other animals. We linked recorded herring-spawn events to complete eBird checklists while accounting for recorded birding effort, observer differences, season, and location structure. Some bird groups and focal species were reported more often or in higher positive counts near recorded spawn, but patterns differed among species, regions, time windows, and response types. Two carefully matched shifted-exposure checks were not statistically prominent after multiplicity adjustment, and WCVI results were usually similar after using a 2-km frame or removing the dominant observer cluster. However, many WCVI sensitivity fits had boundary warnings, and a prespecified Strait of Georgia comparison group was also more frequently detected near spawn. That warning means the data may capture shared season, access, site choice, or submission behavior in addition to ecological responses. The findings describe eligible submitted checklists; they do not prove that spawning caused bird changes or estimate regional bird populations.

## Manuscript highlights

- Registered models linked effort-aware complete eBird checklists to recorded local Pacific herring-spawn exposure without duplicating checklists across concurrent events.
- Guild, focal-species, regional, and event-window associations were heterogeneous; matched WCVI sensitivities usually preserved direction, and no whole-bundle placebo component had BH q below 0.05.
- A non-null Strait of Georgia specificity panel and 43 singular-fit warnings constrain ecological specificity and preclude causal or population-level claims.
